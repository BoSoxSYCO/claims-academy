| A2 | The yard falls from the fence *toward the pool* | Confirmed by video (water visibly runs that way) — high confidence |

| A4 | Soil is sandy and drains fast, but erodes easily | Confirmed by bare sandy lawn + sediment fans — high confidence |
| A5 | You want the least-disruptive fix that actually holds up in FL downpours | Drives the phased approach — do the cheap high-impact work first |

> **The one thing you must confirm — the "garden hose grade test":**
> Run a hose at full flow at the side of the house nearest the fence, and watch where the water goes. Then walk the intended path toward the street. If water naturally trickles toward the street, gravity discharge works (A3 holds). If it pools or runs back toward the pool, we go to the **dry well alternative (§6)** instead of piping to the street. Do this on a dry day before any digging.

### 2. The Strategy in One Picture

```
 BEHIND VINYL FENCE (hill, offsite water)
        │  sheet flow + sand
        ▼
 ┌─────────────────────────────┐  ① INTERCEPTOR (French drain) along fence base
 │  gravel + perforated pipe    │     → catches water BEFORE it hits the lawn
 └───────────────┬─────────────┘
                 │ solid pipe, 1%+ slope
   BACK LAWN     │  ② re-sod + shallow swale  (hold the sand, slow the sheet flow)
  (stabilized)   │
                 ▼
 ┌───────────────────────┐  ③ CATCH BASIN w/ SUMP  (sand settles here, you clean it out)
 │  sediment trap         │
 └───────────┬───────────┘
             │ solid pipe, 1%+ slope, AROUND the pool
             ▼
      ④ DISCHARGE TO STREET (left side)  ── or ──  DRY WELL if hose test fails
 
 POOL DECK: ⑤ upgrade/clean existing trench drain = backstop only
 KNEE WALL: close the gap so nothing shortcuts onto the deck
```

Priority order (do them in this sequence — each stage helps even before the next is built):
**① Intercept → ② Stabilize soil → ③ Trap sediment → ④ Convey to street → ⑤ Deck backstop.**

### 3. Component-by-Component Design & Products

#### ① Interceptor / French drain along the vinyl fence *(highest impact)*
Catches the hill water at the top of the yard before it can sheet across the lawn.

- **Trench:** ~12 in wide × 18–24 in deep, running the full length of the fence where water enters, sloped ~1% toward the street side.
- **Build-up (bottom to top):** non-woven filter fabric lining the trench → 2–3 in of ¾-in washed gravel → **4-in perforated corrugated pipe (holes down)** or a rigid perforated PVC → backfill with washed gravel to ~3 in below grade → wrap fabric over top ("burrito wrap") → cap with 2–3 in of gravel or sod.
- **Why fabric-wrapped:** your sandy soil will clog an unwrapped French drain fast. The fabric is non-negotiable here.
- **Products (generic):** 4-in perforated HDPE drain pipe; 4–6 oz non-woven geotextile filter fabric; ¾-in washed drain rock; **avoid the "fabric sock EZ-flow" no-gravel kits** for this soil — they silt up.

#### ② Re-sod + shallow swale on the back lawn
Stops the sand at the source. Bare sand is what's fouling your pool.

- **Regrade** the lawn to a very shallow swale (a broad, gentle low channel) that guides surface water to the catch basin (③) rather than to the deck.
- **Stabilize:** lay **sod** (fastest — instant cover in FL) over the graded soil; on the steeper pitch near the fence add a **biodegradable erosion-control blanket (straw/coir matting)** pinned down until roots take.
- **Optional low berm** along the top of the knee wall line to steer any surface water toward the swale/basin.

#### ③ Catch basin with sediment sump *(this is what actually protects the pool)*
A settling point where sand drops out and you scoop it up — instead of it reaching the water.

- **Product:** a **12-in (or larger) catch basin box with a deep sump** below the pipe outlet, and an **atrium/beehive grate** (dome grate resists clogging by leaves better than a flat grate). Place it at the low point of the swale, on the fence side of the knee wall.
- The sump lets sand settle below the outlet; you lift the grate and shop-vac/shovel it out a few times a year.
- Tie the interceptor pipe (①) into this basin, and run the outlet toward the street (④).

#### ④ Convey to the street — around the pool, never across it
- **Solid (non-perforated) pipe**, 4-in minimum (6-in if the run is long/flat), **≥1% slope (⅛ in per foot minimum; ¼ in/ft better)**, routed along the side yard to daylight at the street/curb or into a street-side swale.
- **Daylight/pop-up:** a **pop-up emitter** at the discharge end keeps debris and critters out and opens under flow.
- **Corner-lot note (Florida):** discharging to the right-of-way / swale is common but often regulated. **Confirm with your county/city stormwater or code office before connecting to anything in the right-of-way** — see §5.

#### ⑤ Deck trench drain — clean up and keep as backstop
Your deck already has a linear channel drain; in the video it's overwhelmed and silted.

- **Clean it out**, confirm it has a real outlet (not just a soak line that's now clogged), and add a **small catch basin/sump at its low end** so it, too, drops sediment before discharge.
- Tie its outlet into the same conveyance line to the street (④) if grade allows.
- **Close the knee-wall gap** near the spa (mortar/brick to match) so surface flow can no longer shortcut onto the deck — this alone will noticeably cut what reaches the pool.

### 4. Phased Build Plan (do it in stages — money and effort where it counts first)

| Phase | Work | Impact | DIY-able? | Rough cost band* |
|---|---|---|---|---|
| **1** | Close knee-wall gap + clean/verify existing deck drain | Immediate reduction in deck→pool flow | Yes | $ |
| **2** | Re-sod bare lawn + erosion blanket on the slope | Stops most of the *sand* | Yes | $–$$ |
| **3** | Interceptor French drain along fence | Removes the *hill water* at the source | Yes (labor-heavy) or hire | $$ |
| **4** | Catch basin w/ sump + conveyance pipe to street | Routes water off-site, protects pool long-term | Mixed — hire if grade is marginal | $$–$$$ |

\*Relative bands only — get local quotes; sandy soil digs easily, which keeps trenching labor down.

**Even if you stop after Phase 1–2, you'll see a big improvement**, because those two attack the sediment (your worst symptom) directly. Phases 3–4 are what make it hold up in the heaviest storms.

### 5. Permitting / Verification Checklist (Florida corner lot)

- [ ] **Hose grade test (§2)** — confirm gravity flow to the street works.
- [ ] **Call 811 (Sunshine 811)** for underground utility locates **before any digging** — required, free.
- [ ] **Check county/city stormwater rules** for discharging to the right-of-way/swale on a corner lot; some require a permit or prohibit concentrated discharge onto neighbors or the road.
- [ ] **Confirm the property line** behind the vinyl fence so the interceptor stays on your side.
- [ ] **Don't redirect water onto the neighbor** — FL follows reasonable-use rules; discharge to the public street/swale, not the adjacent lot.

### 6. Contingency: if the street isn't a viable outlet (hose test fails)

If water won't gravity-flow to the street, replace component ④ with a **dry well / infiltration pit**: a large gravel-filled or chambered pit (buried) in a low corner of the yard that stores runoff and lets it soak into the sandy soil (which drains well — a point in your favor). The interceptor and catch basin (①–③) stay exactly the same; only the endpoint changes. Sandy Florida soil is well suited to dry wells.
