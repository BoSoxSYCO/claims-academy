# Pool Drainage Improvement Plan
## Report 01 — Site Drainage Assessment & Findings

**Prepared by:** Residential Drainage Engineer (stormwater management, Florida)
**Assessment type:** Visual site assessment from owner-supplied photos and storm-event video
**Date:** 2026-07-18
**Property type:** Single-family residence, **corner lot**
**Subject:** Repeated intrusion of stormwater, sand, and sediment into the swimming pool during heavy rain

---

### 1. Scope & Basis of Assessment

This report covers **Task 1** only: interpretation of site conditions and a recommended drainage *strategy*. It intentionally does **not** specify products, materials, pipe sizes, or a construction detail set — those follow in a later phase once the strategy is confirmed and field measurements/elevations are taken.

Findings are based on:

| Source | What it shows |
|---|---|
| Dry-condition photos (3) | Overall yard layout, pool/spa, decorative knee wall, bare/eroded back lawn, sediment fans on the deck |
| Storm-event video #1 (~22 s) | Active runoff during heavy rain — sheet flow crossing the lawn and pool deck, existing deck trench drain, ponding |
| Storm-event video #2 (~16 s) | Yard-wide ponding and sheet flow off the elevated ground behind the fence line |
| Owner observations | Water enters from behind the white vinyl fence; hill behind the fence; no ditch behind fence; street on the **left**; corner lot |

Slope directions below are inferred from visible water behavior in the storm video (the most reliable evidence), sediment deposition patterns, and standing-water locations. A short instrument survey (or even a line level / laser level check at ~10 key points) should confirm these before final design.

### 2. Site Description

- **Pool & spa:** In-ground pool with an attached raised spa, ringed by a paver/concrete pool deck. The deck sits **lower** than the surrounding back lawn on the fence side.
- **Decorative knee wall:** A low tan brick wall wraps the back/side of the deck in an "L." It is a **landscape/retaining accent — not a water-tight barrier.** It has at least one **stepped opening / gap** (visible near the spa) that concentrates flow onto the deck.
- **Back lawn (fence side):** Largely **bare, sandy soil** with patchy grass — the classic signature of repeated erosion. Sandy Florida soil mobilizes easily.
- **Perimeter:** White vinyl privacy fence along the elevated (uphill) corner, with older wood fence and a chain-link section elsewhere. Neighboring homes/sheds sit on **higher ground** behind the vinyl fence.
- **Existing drainage:** A linear **trench/channel drain already exists in the pool deck.** In the storm video it is clearly **overwhelmed and sediment-laden** — it is capturing some water but is undersized for the volume and has no upstream sediment control.

---

### 3. Findings

#### 3.1 Likely Direction of Runoff

Runoff currently moves in a single dominant path, **toward the pool**:

```
   Hill / higher ground behind WHITE VINYL FENCE   ← ORIGIN (offsite + onsite)
                        │
                        ▼   (sheet flow, gaining sand as it goes)
        Bare sandy BACK LAWN  ← erosion zone
                        │
                        ▼
     Low decorative KNEE WALL (with gap/step-down)  ← flow concentrator
                        │
                        ▼
             POOL DECK (lower than lawn)
                        │
                        ▼
        POOL & SPA  ← LOWEST CATCH POINT (sediment sink)
```

The storm video confirms this directly: water sheets **down and across** the lawn, passes over/through the knee wall, spreads across the deck, and drains **toward the pool**. Runoff has an **offsite component** (the hill behind the fence, with no ditch to intercept it) **plus** the onsite lawn that is graded the wrong way.

#### 3.2 High and Low Points

| Point | Relative elevation | Role |
|---|---|---|
| Ground behind the white vinyl fence (the "hill") | **HIGHEST** | Source of offsite runoff; no interception |
| Back lawn between fence and knee wall | High → mid (slopes down toward deck) | Erosion / sediment source |
| Top of decorative knee wall | Mid (a partial, leaky ridge) | Should be a barrier; currently bypassed at gap |
| Pool deck | **LOW** | Sheet-flow conveyor onto the water |
| **Pool & spa water surface** | **LOWEST on site** | Everything currently drains here |
| The **street** (left side of lot) | Lower than the yard, and the legal/intended discharge | **Where water *should* go — but the yard does not currently grade to it** |

The core geometry problem: **the pool is the low point of the backyard, and the street — the correct outlet — is on the opposite side from where water is entering.** Water has no path to the street, so it collects at the pool.

#### 3.3 Erosion Areas

1. **Back lawn (fence side) — primary erosion zone.** Bare, sandy, patchy — soil is being stripped and carried downslope with each storm.
2. **Knee-wall gap / step-down near the spa — sediment deposition zone.** Close-up photos show **sand ripple/dune deposits (a sediment fan) on the pavers** right where flow slows after passing the wall. This is textbook: erosion uphill, deposition where velocity drops, and the finest sediment carries the last few feet **into the pool.**
3. **Along the base of the knee wall and gravel bed**, where an existing loose corrugated pipe sits — evidence of a prior, inadequate drainage attempt that is not functioning.

#### 3.4 Why Flooding (and Sediment Intrusion) Is Occurring

Five compounding causes:

1. **Adverse grading.** The backyard slopes **toward the pool**, not toward the street. The pool is the lowest point, so it acts as the catch basin for the whole yard.
2. **Uncontrolled offsite flow.** The hill behind the vinyl fence drains onto the property with **no interception** (owner confirms no ditch behind the fence). This offsite water arrives as sheet flow across the full fence line.
3. **The knee wall is not a water barrier.** It is decorative, sits *below* the upstream grade in places, and has a **gap/step-down** that concentrates flow directly onto the deck.
4. **Erodible sandy soil with poor vegetative cover.** Bare sand has little resistance; it mobilizes readily and is the sediment being deposited on the deck and into the pool.
5. **Undersized, unprotected existing drainage.** The single deck trench drain is overwhelmed in heavy rain and has **no upstream sediment pre-treatment**, so whatever sand reaches it either clogs it or bypasses it into the pool.

In short: **too much water arriving from the high side, aimed the wrong way (at the pool instead of the street), carrying sand that has nothing stopping it.**

---

### 4. Recommended Drainage Strategy (Conceptual)

The strategy is a classic **"intercept high, convey around, discharge low"** stormwater approach, adapted so the pool is *taken out of the flow path entirely* and sediment is stopped *before* it can reach the water. Four layers, in priority order:

**1. Intercept at the high side (behind / along the vinyl fence).**
Capture offsite hill runoff **before it reaches the lawn** with a continuous interception line running along the base of the fence. This is the single highest-impact move — it removes the offsite volume at the source and keeps it off the erodible lawn.

**2. Redirect and convey toward the street — around the pool, never across it.**
Establish a positive-drainage path (regraded swale and/or a piped collection line) that carries intercepted water **along the side of the property to the street on the left**, which is the natural and legal low-point outlet for a corner lot. The guiding principle: **water should be routed around the pool deck, not over it.**

**3. Stop sediment before the deck — protect the pool.**
Because the soil is sandy, sediment control is mandatory, not optional. The strategy uses **stabilized ground cover on the bare lawn** (re-establish dense turf or planted/mulched cover to hold the sand in place) **plus a sediment-capture point** (a sump/settling feature) upstream of any drain so sand drops out and settles where it can be cleaned out — **never in the pool.** Close the knee-wall gap so flow can no longer shortcut onto the deck.

**4. Upgrade on-deck drainage as a backstop, not the primary defense.**
The existing deck trench drain stays as a **secondary safety net** for the small amount of direct rainfall on the deck itself — but it is deliberately *not* relied on to handle the whole yard. Fixing the upstream grading and interception is what solves the problem; the deck drain just polishes it.

**Priority ranking:** (1) intercept the hill runoff → (2) regrade/convey to the street → (3) stabilize soil + trap sediment → (4) improve deck drainage. Doing #1 and #2 well is ~80% of the solution.

### 5. Recommended Next Steps

1. **Confirm elevations** at ~8–12 points (fence line, lawn, top of knee wall, deck, and toward the street) with a laser/line level to verify the slope directions inferred here and to confirm the street is a viable gravity outlet.
2. **Verify the discharge point at the street** is permissible (local/HOA/municipal stormwater rules for corner lots in Florida — discharge to the right-of-way often has requirements).
3. **Locate the property line behind the vinyl fence** so the interception line stays on-property.
4. Once the above are confirmed, proceed to **Task 2 — product selection, sizing, and a construction detail set** for each of the four strategy layers.
